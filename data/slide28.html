      'overflow' creates a new formatting context, this way the element with this 'overflow' style will be isolated from the outer context. This isolation allows clearing the previous context: nexted elements with 'float' can expand their parent.
      • Pros:
      • - simple code and adequate behavior
      • Cons:
      • - Nested elements with absolute positioning will be cut short or hidden, if they are located outside the bounderies of the element.
    • The 'Floating Clearing' techniques-3:
      'clearfix' Psuedo-element ':after' is invisible, with zero-height, and is in fact missing in the document stream thanks to its 'display: block; it will take up the whole of parent's width because of 'clear: both;' and the 'clear : both;' will clear out the document stream.
      • Pros:
      • - no need to create a separate block element with the style of 'clear: both;'
      • - nested absolute-positioned elements will not be cut short
      • Cons:
      • - The element still remains in the overall document stream: its nested elements' paddings and margins will cave in under the element into the 'float', if such a nested element exists.
    • The 'Floating Clearing' techniques-4:
      'display:table' just like 'overflow: hidden;' creates its own formatting context, but does not cut short the absolute-positioned elements that go over its bounds.
      • Pros:
      • - does not cut short the elements
      • - better behavior than that of 'clearfix'
      • Cons:
      • - elemtn will cut short its width by context to show behavior of a table
      • - to resolve the last drawback:
      Because of the 2 dots in ':after' and the 10px white-space between them, browser will always display this element at the size of the table, and not the overall size of the screen. The fact this element hight is zero, will hide the dots.
